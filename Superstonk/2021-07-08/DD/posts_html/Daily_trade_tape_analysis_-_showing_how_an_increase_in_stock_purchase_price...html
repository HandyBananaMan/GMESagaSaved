Ok, now you know which stocks, let's talk about what exactly I am looking at here. While watching GME over the past months, I have become increasingly interested in all of the trades that fly by every second. They can go by so fast at times that you don't even have a moment to process the information. With that in mind, I started dumping the data out to a file so I could then review and chart it. I had started looking at the 1 second after open and 2 seconds after close trades but over time I didn't feel like that was telling me anything. Thinking about it some more, I started looking at the sequence of trades and I noticed something interesting (to me at least). Many of the trades on the tape seemed to have a subsequent trade which immediately negated any price increase from the sale just before it. Don't worry, I HAVE LOTS OF PICTURES for you to look at. Hang with me.

So what do I mean by a trade has a subsequent trade negating the price increase? I have seen many times where a trade will hit the tape with a $x gain vs the trade immediately before it. Ok, you say, that doesn't mean anything other than the trade price went up $X. You are a smart ape for thinking that. However, if you look closer, you will likely see another trade which follows immediately after the +$x, but it will be for -$x. The $x gain on the 1st trade is immediately flipped to a -$x decrease on a subsequent trade. Putting real numbers to this example, let's say trade #200 on the day showed in increase of +$.02 over trade #199, you very likely will see trade #201 come along and be -$.02 when compared to trade #200, resulting in the "last trade" value dropping back down to the value of trade #199. Making any sense yet? My head hurts, hope yours doesn't. Oh yeah, one other thing. The negating trade value doesn't always happen in just one trade. Sometimes it's two trades added together which creates the total necessary to negate the $x increase. I PROMISE I HAVE PRETTY PICTURES!!

Why are we talking about this again? Good question. Everyone here knows that the market is manipulated by those that apes love to hate, that's no surprise. What I was surprised by is how often a positive movement is immediately negated by a negative movement.
